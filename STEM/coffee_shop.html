Overview

In this game you're responsible for managing a coffee shop. Responsibilities include keeping your inventory stocked and selecting the best price to charge your customers for your coffee. You'll need to keep your funds high enough that you can afford to open your shop each day. If you don't have enough funds to open, you can sell either some of your coffee or some of your cups back to your supplier. Keep in mind the supplier will buy back the items at half the price you bought them for, so it's a situation you'll want to do your best to avoid.

Analysis

Let's break down how this game works. Each day your shop will be visited by between 10 and 20 potential customers. The fraction of those people who decide to buy a coffee depends on the price of coffee that you've set. The plot below illustrates this behaviour. I created this plot by playing the game for 50 days at a coffee price of 3 dollars, then averaging the customer-visitor ratio from that run. Then I reset the game, incremented the coffee price and repeated the process. After repeating that 18 more times (to be clear, I wrote a script that automated the process for me), I had the average customer-visitor ratio for whole number coffee prices between 3 and 22. You can see that for very low coffee prices, the customer-visitor ratio is exactly 1. Then, the fraction of visitors who buy a coffee begins to decrease as the unit price of the coffee goes up. The customer-visitor ratio continues to decrease towards the horizontal asymptote along the x axis until the price of the coffee is so high that the probability of a visitor choosing to buy a coffee is statistically negligible.

So, if you set the price of your coffee to 2 dollars, every visitor you get will be a customer (or in other words, the customer-visitor ratio that day will be 1). Yay! But wait... don't celebrate too early. Your supplier charges you 2 dollars per cup and 3 dollars for the coffee required to fill that cup, meaning you'd be selling each coffee at a loss of 3 dollars. Conversely, if you set the price of your coffee to 18 dollars, after factoring in the cost of materials you'd be selling each coffee at a gain of 12 dollars. However your customer-visitor ratio will be very low. With the added fact that it costs 20 dollars to open your shop everyday, this is a risky business model. The plot below shows that there is a sweet spot for the price where the unit gain of selling a cup of coffee combines with a sufficiently high customer-visitor ratio to produce a coffee shop will break even (on average).

This plot was created using a similar process as the customer-visitor ratio plot. I ran the game for 1000 days with a coffee price of 6 dollars, then averaged the daily profit over the 1000 days. Then I reset the game, incremented the price to 7 dollars and repeated the process (again, I used automation to speed up the process). You can see that the coffee shop showed a net positive profit for coffee prices between 9 and 12 dollars.
